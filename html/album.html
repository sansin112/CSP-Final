Taylor Swift (2006)

    Swift's debut album, Taylor Swift, was made available on October 24th, 2006. She released five singles to promote the album and self-penned 11 of the songs. Nearly immediately after the album's release, the artist became famous, and the record lasted 24 weeks at the top of the Billboard 200's Country Albums chart. Acoustic guitars, violins, and banjos are given free rein as they strum along to stories of childhood pals who would later fall in love, summer romances that wouldn't last, and backroad encounters. "Picture To Burn" is a disgruntled teen's "Before He Cheats," substituting shooting whiskey for gossip websites, with the humor and skill of a seasoned musician. As one of the three songs she wrote entirely on her own, "Should've Said No" carries the same flame with a steadier touch.

Fearless (2008)

    The public didn't really grasp Taylor Swift's artistic potential until 2008's Fearless, although her debut album assured she would at the very least be a passing star. The songs "Love Story" and "You Belong with Me," which became immediate classics regardless of how much her style and tastes have changed, are the ones that (for the first time) cemented her place in the cultural zeitgeist. Therefore, it made logical for this, her second studio album, to serve as the first altar in the "Taylor's Version" re-recording tale. This album garnered her four Grammys, including Album of the Year.

Speak Now (2010)

    When Taylor Swift, at 20 years old, published Speak Now, it featured her storytelling abilities. With heightened production from soon-to-be-mainstay producer Jack Antonoff, who uses an undercurrent of drums that boom like palms on metal doors and vocals that echo as if off skyscrapers, she fully transitioned from country music in 1989 after flirting with it in Red two years before. The album discusses the line that blurs between maturity and childhood as well as Taylor's captivating talent for turning any memory — no matter how charming or difficult — into a scene that seems like your own.

Red (2012)

    Without a question, Taylor's most divisive work to date is Reputation. Taylor eventually abandons her ten-year-old good girl reputation in favor of the villain alter-ego created by the media when Kanye started a raging Twitter rant, choosing to settle the score by doing what she does best: exploring new aural terrain. She abandons her cheery synth-pop this time in favor of overt trap vibrations and heavy-handed hip-hop, which sound like loud emergency strobes disturbing a pitch-black cityscape. When it was first released in 2017, many listeners weren't very impressed by what they perceived as fake-edgy antics, the equivalent of a prep wearing black lipstick on a bad day.

1989 (2004)

    Taylor's 1989 album represents something of a comeback, not just because it is titled after the year she was born, but also because it marked her first significant crossover from country to pop music. Instead, 1989 is sonically exuberant without sacrificing depth or relying too much on familiarity. However, there is always a hollow foil track from 1989 for every genuinely outstanding song, with "Shake It Off" and "Bad Blood" being the biggest offenders. Between those two extremes, there is a steady stream of incredibly catchy mega-hits that spread through Top 40 countdowns and later TikTok videos.

Lover (2019)

    In some ways, Lover is a regress. The follow-up to Reputation, Lover, is a blushing cheek shining with pink reflection and aspirations for the future, whereas Reputation before it was a dangerous, aggressive side-step hidden in a smokey eye. Taylor again reverts to her tried-and-true toolkit, leaning on romantic impulse and heavenly daydreams: living room waltzes, paper promise rings, creeping through gardens, and bouncing down streets. This may be Taylor's way of making up for that previous album's wave of unfavorable publicity.
